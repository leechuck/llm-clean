Background. OntoClean provides a principled methodology for detecting
awkward taxonomic decisions in ontologies, but its application
requires manual annotation of every class with five modal
meta-properties (rigidity, identity, own-identity, unity, dependence),
a cost that has kept it out of routine use on biomedical ontologies,
exactly where the analysis would be most informative.

Method. Here, we present a workflow that uses a fine-tuned large
language model (LoRA-adapted Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct) to assign the five
OntoClean meta-properties automatically. We classify the hierarchy
with the ELK reasoner and check OntoClean constraints (R1–R3, I1,
U1–U2, O1) on every inferred (sub, super) pair. A second LLM pass
audits each violation as a real defect, a downstream cascade, or a
predictor error.

Results. Applied to the Emotion Ontology (64 classes), the workflow
surfaces 303 violations, including a plausible rigidity conflict on
the GO chain "sweat secretion ⊑ secretion by tissue" and a contested
unity assignment on "phenomenal cognitive experience". The audit
attributes 83.5% of the violations to cascades from a few upstream
mislabelings on GO/BFO process classes, where the model over-assigns
identity to occurrents. We show that this limitation is reducible at
zero training cost: re-running inference with a BFO-grounded system
prompt cuts violations from 303 to 72 (−76.2%), removes the entire R2
cluster, and collapses the residue to four specific mislabeled classes.

Conclusion. LLM-assigned OntoClean is a viable route to scaling
principled ontology evaluation across biomedicine. Aggregate violation
counts should not serve as quality signals without auditing; full
removal of cascades will require BFO-aware fine-tuning.
